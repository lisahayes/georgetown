A wet yard is a question before it is a repair

Standing water or a soggy patch in the yard is a symptom, not a diagnosis. Underground, four separate systems could be responsible: the main water line feeding the house, the irrigation system, the sewer lateral carrying waste away, or, if you have one, the pool plumbing. Each leaks water into the same ground, but each needs a completely different repair, so the first and most important job is naming the culprit.

Guessing here is expensive. Digging where the ground is wettest often misses the leak entirely, because water travels underground before it surfaces. We sort out which system is losing water before a shovel touches the yard.

Reading the clues the yard gives

The character of the wet spot points the way. A clean, soggy patch that appears in dry weather, with no smell, usually means a pressurized water or irrigation line. A wet area carrying a sewage odor, often worse after the house has been in use, points to the sewer lateral. Water pooling near a pool deck suggests pool plumbing. And a patch that only appears during or after an irrigation cycle points at the sprinklers or drip rather than the main line.

Timing matters too. A leak that runs constantly behaves differently than one tied to when you run water, irrigate, or use the pool, and that pattern helps us separate the systems.

Confirming the source

Once the likely system is identified, we confirm it with the right test. We isolate the water service at the meter and watch whether the leak continues, which separates the house supply from irrigation. We camera the sewer lateral if the signs point there. We pressure-test irrigation zones or pool lines as needed. Each test rules a system in or out, so we end up certain rather than hopeful.

Only after the system is confirmed do we locate the leak to a spot along that line, so any excavation is targeted to the actual break.

Repairing the right system

From there the repair matches the system: a spliced or replaced water service line, an irrigation valve or lateral, a sewer lateral repair, or a pool line fix. Because we confirmed the source first, you are not paying to open the yard for the wrong pipe. Where a line crosses a driveway or mature landscaping, a trenchless approach can keep the surface damage small.

We retest after the repair to confirm the yard stops taking on water, which closes the loop on a problem that started as a mystery.

Why a wet yard is worth sorting out now

A leak under the yard does not stay in the yard. A water or irrigation leak wastes water and money every day, a sewer leak is a health concern as well as a nuisance, and any of them can erode soil, undermine a slab or a deck, and kill the landscaping above. The longer the source goes unnamed, the more the ground around it shifts and settles.

If part of your Georgetown yard stays wet, sinks, or smells, that is worth diagnosing before it spreads to the foundation or the hardscape.

Questions

Yard Leak questions, answered

What could be leaking under my yard?
Four systems are possible: the main water line, the irrigation system, the sewer lateral, or pool plumbing. Each leaks into the same ground but needs a different repair, so we identify which one before any digging.
How do you tell which system is leaking in the yard?
By the clues and the tests. A clean soggy patch points to a water or irrigation line, a sewage smell points to the sewer, and pool-side water points to pool plumbing. We then isolate the meter, camera the sewer, or pressure-test lines to confirm.
Why not just dig where the yard is wettest?
Because water travels underground before it surfaces, so the wettest spot is often not the break. We locate the leak along the confirmed line first, which keeps the dig targeted instead of hit-or-miss.
